ADHD: Understanding the Mind

This article combines informational awareness with personal perspective.

By mayBlog Editorial April 2026 5 min read

ADHD is a condition that affects attention, impulse control, and energy levels. It can make everyday tasks feel harder than they seem to others, even when the person is trying their best.

I didn’t realize for the longest time that what I was dealing with had a name. I just thought I was lazy, distracted, or somehow not trying hard enough. In school, I would sit down with full intention to study, but within minutes my mind would drift. Sometimes I’d be staring at the same page for half an hour, not absorbing a single word. Other times, I’d suddenly get a burst of energy and finish hours of work in one sitting. It felt inconsistent, unpredictable—like I had no control over my own focus. Later, I came across the term ADHD—Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder—and things started to make a little more sense. It’s not just about being “hyper” or unable to sit still. For me, it’s more like a constant battle between what I need to do and what my brain wants to do. Even simple tasks can feel overwhelming, especially when there’s no immediate reward or urgency attached to them.

Knowing the reason behind the struggle can be the first step toward healing.

One of the hardest parts is how it affects daily life in ways that other people don’t always see. I forget small things—where I put my keys, what someone just told me, deadlines that seemed clear a day ago. I start multiple tasks and finish very few. And then there’s the frustration that comes with it. You know what you’re capable of, but you can’t seem to access it consistently. At the same time, there are moments where everything clicks. I can hyperfocus on something I’m interested in for hours without even noticing time passing. Those moments feel amazing, but they also make the inconsistency more confusing.
Why can I focus so deeply sometimes, but not when it actually matters? Understanding ADHD hasn’t magically solved everything, but it has helped me be less harsh on myself. Instead of calling myself lazy, I try to work with how my brain functions—breaking tasks into smaller steps, setting reminders, and giving myself structure where I can. It’s still a work in progress, but at least now I know I’m not just failing for no reason. There’s a pattern to it, and slowly, I’m learning how to handle it.
